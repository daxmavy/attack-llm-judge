\begin{abstract}
% TL;DR: gap robust under trimmed mean; rank reversal on naive mean is one-judge artefact.
Clarity ratings from large language models are now load-bearing in
evaluation pipelines, and rewriting a paragraph to raise its score is
a cheap attack. The natural defence is to change the model that serves
as judge. We ask how much an attacker's score inflation survives that
swap, and how much of the surviving gain reflects genuine quality
improvement rather than gaming of the attacker's chosen proxy. Ten
inference-only rewriters of UK political-opinion paragraphs attack a
two-judge \emph{proxy} panel (Qwen-2.5-7B, Llama-3.1-8B). Each rewrite
is then re-scored on a disjoint five-judge \emph{gold} panel (Haiku-4.5,
Gemini-2.5-Flash, GPT-5-mini, DeepSeek-V3.2, Llama-4-Maverick) the
attacker never sees. A DeBERTa stance regressor trained on the same
corpus keeps the fidelity axis visible. Two findings fall out. First,
the attack panel over-states gold-panel uplift by a robust margin:
$6.1$--$7.4$ clarity points across three methods (paired,
proposition-cluster bootstrap, $n \geq 481$) when the gold panel is
aggregated by trimmed mean (drop the highest and lowest of five).
Second, switching to an un-trimmed mean of the same five judges
inflates one method's gap to $11.4$ points and flips the method
ranking between the two panels. A single gold judge (DeepSeek-V3.2)
scores that method $\sim 22$ points below its peers and carries the
un-trimmed mean with it. The transfer gap is real and roughly uniform across methods;
the rank reversal is a statistical artefact of a non-robust
aggregator. Defenders who average a small gold panel should trim;
attackers who optimise against a small proxy panel will over-state
their reach.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

% TL;DR: LLM judges are everywhere; swapping the judge is the natural defence.
Large language models increasingly stand in for human evaluators when
judging text: AlpacaEval, MT-Bench, Arena-Hard, RewardBench, and
rubric-based leaderboards all route decisions through an LLM's
$0$--$100$ verdict \citep{liu2023geval,kim2024prometheus2,liu2025judgesverdict}.
The rubric is usually public, the judge's weights are not, and a
deployer can swap models at any time. An attacker who rewrites content
to raise its score has, at best, query access to a judge the deployer
is free to change.

% TL;DR: proxy-vs-gold divergence is well-studied for RMs, not rewrite attacks.
Two settings have looked at whether rewriting attacks transfer across
judges. \citet{raina2024} learn universal adversarial suffixes that
generalise across FlanT5, Llama, Mistral, and GPT-3.5. \citet{kaneko2026paa}
iteratively paraphrase scientific abstracts and measure
matched-to-mismatched score drop across three frontier LLM reviewers.
Neither compares attacker uplift to a \emph{gold} reference panel
outside the attacker's feedback loop. The closest precedent lives in
the reward-model-overoptimisation literature
\citep{gao2023,coste2024,eisenstein2023}: reinforcement learning
against a proxy reward model deviates from a held-out gold reward
model as optimisation proceeds. To our knowledge that setup has not
been ported to rewriting attacks on LLM judges.

% TL;DR: we run the joint experiment; stance preservation is the fidelity axis.
We ran that experiment. The setting is clarity scoring of
UK political-opinion paragraphs from the pre-registered
\paulcorpus corpus \citep{pauldata2025}.
Ten inference-only rewriter methods, all built on
Qwen-2.5-72B-Instruct, attack a two-judge proxy panel whose models the
attacker is permitted to query during optimisation. Each rewrite is
then re-scored on a disjoint five-judge gold panel whose models the
attacker never sees. Stance preservation, measured with a
DeBERTa-v3-base regressor trained on the same corpus
(Pearson $0.94$; \cref{sec:method}), supplies a domain-specific
fidelity axis that none of the prior rewriting-attack papers used.

% TL;DR: central finding + Fig 1 forward pointer.
\Cref{fig:transfer} is the paper in one image. Red circles mark each
method's uplift when the gold panel is aggregated by mean of all five
judges; blue squares mark the same method under trimmed mean
(drop the highest and lowest of the five). Under the naive mean, one
method's gap (\texttt{lit\_inf\_t}, a literature-informed prompt
aggregating best practice from GEO
\citep{aggarwal2024geo}, Prometheus \citep{kim2024prometheus2}, and
G-Eval \citep{liu2023geval}) stretches
to $11.4$ clarity points---enough to reverse the method ranking
between the two panels. Under the trimmed mean the same method's gap
shrinks to $7.4$, within a point of every other method's gap
($6.1$ and $7.1$), and the rank reversal disappears.
\Cref{fig:perjudge} explains the asymmetry: a single gold judge
(DeepSeek-V3.2) scores \texttt{lit\_inf\_t} $\sim 22$ points below
every other method while agreeing with its peers on the other
rewriters. The un-trimmed mean inherits that outlier; the trimmed
mean does not.

% TL;DR: bullet contributions (canonical per Brendan / how-to-ML).
\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
  \item For inference-only rewriting attacks on LLM judges, we
  measure a paired \gap\ against a disjoint five-model gold
  panel. Under trimmed-mean aggregation the gap is $6.1$, $7.1$, and
  $7.4$ clarity points across three methods ($95\%$ CIs
  $[5.2, 6.8]$, $[6.0, 8.2]$, $[6.1, 8.6]$)---real, large, and
  roughly uniform.
  \item We show an apparent rank reversal between the proxy and
  gold panels under naive mean-of-five gold aggregation
  (Spearman $\rho = -1$ across the three complete methods). That
  reversal is driven by a single gold judge, DeepSeek-V3.2, which
  scores one method $22$ points below its peers. Trimmed-mean
  aggregation removes the reversal while preserving the transfer
  gap.
  \item The methodological consequence is sharp: small gold panels
  aggregated by non-robust statistics manufacture method-specific
  differences that do not survive a single-outlier-resistant
  estimator. We recommend trimmed mean (or median) for any gold
  panel smaller than ten judges.
  \item We release an append-only evaluation suite
  (SQLite schema, metric modules, bootstrap code), the judge rubric,
  all rewriter prompts, the panel slugs, and the stance and clarity
  regressors needed to reproduce the analysis.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

% TL;DR: rewriting attacks with a transfer axis -- PAA and RobustJudge.
\paragraph{Rewriting attacks with a cross-judge axis.}
\citet{kaneko2026paa} run the closest-in-spirit experiment. Their
\emph{Paraphrasing Adversarial Attack} iteratively rewrites ICLR
abstracts under a BERTScore and perplexity drift constraint; the
reviewers are GPT-4o, Gemini-2.5-Pro, and Sonnet-4. Their
matched-reviewer mean score is $4.3$; mismatched drops to $3.5$;
baseline is $2.7$. Half of the matched uplift survives the model swap.
Two substantive differences. First, PAA has no gold reference outside
the attack's optimisation loop; its mismatched reviewers are frontier
LLMs judging a rubric they were never given. Our gold panel shares
the rubric with the attack panel and sits above it in capability, so
our gap measures over-optimisation rather than cross-model
disagreement about what the rubric means. Second, PAA's fidelity axis
(BERTScore $\geq 0.85$) ignores stance. A stance flip inside that
threshold is invisible to PAA and visible to us. \citet{wu2025robustjudge} catalogue fifteen attacks and introduce
\emph{iSDR}, which subtracts a task-specific quality metric
(BLEURT for text, CodeBLEU for code) from the attack's raw score gain.
iSDR is the closest published precedent for quality-adjusted uplift,
but each task gets a single hand-picked quality metric; our
five-judge gold panel is a higher-variance, lower-bias substitute.

% TL;DR: non-rewriting attacks on LLM judges -- suffix and prompt injection.
\paragraph{Non-rewriting attacks on LLM judges.}
\citet{raina2024} learn a four-token suffix on FlanT5-3B that
maximises scores on Llama-2-7B, Mistral-7B, and GPT-3.5, irrespective
of the input content: the first large-scale demonstration that
absolute-scoring judges are catastrophically vulnerable to transferable
surface attacks. \citet{shi2024judgedeceiver} optimise an injected suffix inside one
candidate response for a pairwise judge and get $99\%$ attack-success
transfer from Llama-3-8B to Llama-2-13B. Both are the strongest prior
evidence that transfer happens, but both attack surfaces are
non-semantic suffixes. They do not speak to whether disciplined
rewriting attacks transfer.

% TL;DR: RM overoptimisation -- methodological lineage for gold panel.
\paragraph{Reward-model overoptimisation.}
The methodology we apply is adapted from the
\mbox{proxy-vs-gold} reward-model literature.
\citet{gao2023} establish the canonical curve: optimisation against a
proxy reward model raises proxy-measured reward monotonically while
gold-RM reward eventually reverses. \citet{coste2024} and
\citet{eisenstein2023} show that ensembling proxy RMs blunts but does
not eliminate the effect. \citet{eisenstein2023} also show that
\emph{pretraining-seed} diversity matters more than fine-tuning-seed
diversity, which is why our proxy panel pairs a Qwen model with a
Llama model rather than two Qwen checkpoints. \citet{laidlaw2024correlated}
formalise reward hacking as breakdown of proxy-gold correlation under
optimisation. The limitation for our purposes is that the entire
lineage studies reward models, not LLM judges, and defender-side, not
attacker-side. We are porting its diagnostic across both axes.

% TL;DR: reward hacking during iterative refinement -- ICRH.
\paragraph{Reward hacking during iterative refinement.}
\citet{pan2024icrh} document in-context reward hacking in iterative
self-refinement of essays: the in-loop judge's score rises
monotonically while a held-out judge and human raters diverge from it.
Our \texttt{icir\_single} method is designed to stress this exact
setting; unlike \citeauthor{pan2024icrh}, we put a full gold panel, not
a single held-out judge, on the other side of the loop.

% TL;DR: training-based rewriter attacks -- the training-agent's neighbourhood.
\paragraph{Training-based rewriter attacks.}
Three papers train rewriting policies against reward signals that are
related to judges. \citet{ziegenbein2024argmt,ziegenbein2026grpo} train
rewriters on argument appropriateness with classifier and GRPO
rewards; neither paper includes a held-out judge or a gold panel.
\citet{chen2025autogeo} train a Qwen3-1.7B rewriter with GRPO for
generative-search-engine visibility, using a compound reward; rule
overlap across extractor LLMs is $78$--$84\%$. These papers define
the shelf our training-based arm (GRPO, with SFT on BoN-selected
pairs, \cref{sec:training}) sits on. What they lack is the
unknown-judge-and-gold-decomposition protocol.

% TL;DR: format / length / single-token attacks -- peripheral but necessary.
\paragraph{Format and length bias.}
Length bias in LLM judges is documented by \citet{dubois2024alpacaeval}
(regression-based debiasing for AlpacaEval), and format bias by
\citet{zhang2024format}. \citet{zhao2025onetoken} report that single
tokens (\texttt{``:''}, \texttt{``Thought process:''}) inflate
generative-RM scores across many judges. Our \texttt{injection\_leadin} method calibrates how much of that
surface failure mode reaches a rubric-scored clarity rating at the
$8$B judge scale. \Cref{sec:results} shows it gains $7.5$ gold
points over the source, close to \texttt{naive} paraphrase and
within sampling noise of the $\pm 10\%$ length retry.

% TL;DR: what exactly we are NOT claiming.
\paragraph{What we do not claim.}
We are not the first to measure cross-judge transfer
(\citealp{raina2024,kaneko2026paa,wu2025robustjudge}). We are not the
first to decompose attacker uplift against a quality reference
(\citealp{wu2025robustjudge}'s iSDR, the RM lineage). Our contribution
is the \emph{conjunction}: rewriting attacks, cross-judge transfer, a
disjoint gold LLM panel, and stance preservation as the domain-specific
fidelity axis. No single prior study spans those four axes, and the
rank-reversal and single-judge-driver findings
(\cref{sec:results}) are not predicted by any of them individually.

\section{Background and problem setting}
\label{sec:problem}

\subsection{LLM-as-judge, in formalism}
% TL;DR: rubric-based scoring, one judge produces one score per paragraph.
A \emph{rubric} $R$ is a natural-language scoring instruction with
explicit anchors at $\{0, 20, 40, 60, 80, 100\}$ for a criterion $C$
(here: clarity of expression). A \emph{judge} $J$ is an LLM that given
$(R, x)$ produces an integer score $s(x) \in \{0, \ldots, 100\}$
together with a short reasoning trace; we ignore the trace. A panel
$\mathcal{P} = \{J_1, \ldots, J_k\}$ applied to paragraph $x$
yields $s_{\mathcal{P}}(x) = \tfrac{1}{k}\sum_i s_{J_i}(x)$.

\subsection{Threat model}
% TL;DR: attacker = query N proxies, rewrite content, no gold access.
An attacker holds a corpus $\mathcal{X}$ of source paragraphs and a
rewriter policy $\pi$. During optimisation the attacker may query any
judge in a \emph{proxy panel} $\mathcal{P}_\text{proxy}$ of their
choosing (here $|\mathcal{P}_\text{proxy}| = 2$). The attacker picks
that panel before any gold feedback. At evaluation time a
\emph{gold panel} $\mathcal{P}_\text{gold}$
(here $|\mathcal{P}_\text{gold}| = 5$) re-scores every
rewrite; the attacker never sees these scores in training, selection,
or reward. All judges share rubric $R$ and a
fixed system prompt. On the defender side no filter, detector, or
prompt randomisation is deployed; only the model choice. This is not
the strongest attacker possible; a black-box setting where the rubric
itself is hidden would be strictly harder and is out of scope.

\subsection{The \gap and the aggregation choice}
% TL;DR: gap definition with two aggregators; trimmed mean is robust against a single judge.
For each rewrite $y = \pi(x)$ we define the paired \gap as
\[
  \gap(y) \;=\; \bigl[s_{\mathcal{P}_\text{proxy}}(y) - s_{\mathcal{P}_\text{proxy}}(x)\bigr]
              - \bigl[A_{\mathcal{P}_\text{gold}}(y) - A_{\mathcal{P}_\text{gold}}(x)\bigr],
\]
where $A_{\mathcal{P}}$ is a panel aggregator. We report two:
$A_\text{mean}(x) = \tfrac{1}{k} \sum_i s_{J_i}(x)$ (mean over the five
gold judges) and $A_\text{trim}(x)$, the mean after dropping the
highest and lowest of the five scores. For $k{=}5$ the trimmed mean
is the least-influenced common estimator with breakdown point $1/k$.
A positive \gap\ means the proxy panel perceives a bigger improvement
than the gold panel does. Reporting both aggregators is deliberate:
$A_\text{mean}$ is what a deployer reads off by default;
$A_\text{trim}$ is the answer after one judge's
outlier is put aside.

\subsection{Stance preservation as a fidelity axis}
% TL;DR: political-opinion domain requires stance regressor, not BERTScore.
The paragraphs we rewrite express a political position. Semantic
similarity alone does not detect a stance flip---a rewrite can
paraphrase verbatim and reverse the author's conclusion. We use a
DeBERTa-v3-base regressor trained on the \paulcorpus human-rated
agreement scores (Pearson $0.94$, MAE $0.08$ on held-out AI model
paragraphs) to estimate $\hat{\alpha}(y)$, the post-rewrite agreement
score, and track $|\hat{\alpha}(y) - \hat{\alpha}(x)|$ as stance drift.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Corpus and sampling}
% TL;DR: paul_data, 100 propositions, stratified 20% subsample of 4503 writers.
The \paulcorpus corpus contains $4{,}503$ human-written paragraphs
(\emph{writers}) on $100$ UK political or policy propositions, rated
for clarity, informativeness, and agreement score by $\sim 30$ human
raters each. We evaluate on a $20\%$ stratified subsample
(tag \texttt{main\_20pct}, $903$ writers): five per-proposition
agreement-score quintiles crossed with a top-decile flag for the
highest-clarity writer in each (proposition, quintile) cell.
Proportional sampling preserves both the per-proposition stance
distribution and the top-decile fraction.

\subsection{Judge panels}
% TL;DR: 2 proxies, 5 gold, cross-family, frontier tier for gold.
\Cref{tab:panels} lists the live OpenRouter model slugs used for
every judge call. The proxy panel pairs a Qwen and a Llama model at
matched parameter tier; the gold panel takes one model from five
different labs. All judges use the same rubric prompt. Models marked
as reasoning models (GPT-5-mini, internally used Gemini-2.5-Pro during
pilot) emit hidden reasoning tokens; the judge client sets
\texttt{reasoning.effort=low} and raises \texttt{max\_tokens} to $500$
so the visible answer fits.

\subsection{Training-based arm (ongoing)}
\label{sec:training}
% TL;DR: stub for training arm results.
An accompanying GRPO run fine-tunes a Qwen-2.5-1.5B-Instruct rewriter
against $r = \tfrac{1}{2}(s_\text{qwen7b} + s_\text{llama8b})$ on a
disjoint $200$-prompt train / $50$-prompt eval split of top-decile
writer paragraphs. \cref{app:grpo} documents the TRL configuration
(group size $G{=}4$, effective batch $32$, learning rate
$5{\times}10^{-6}$, KL coefficient $0.01$) and the vLLM-colocated
rollout infrastructure. A single-fold $25$-step reference run raises
the mean proxy reward from $74$ to $85$. A parallel SFT-on-BoN arm is
planned. We report training-arm gold-panel scores in
\cref{sec:training-results} once the runs complete; the eval-side
infrastructure is already identical to the inference-only methods and
the gold re-scoring will drop into the same tables without further
code changes.

\subsection{Drift and detection}
% TL;DR: stance, length, detection, clarity regressor -- 5 axes.
Every rewrite is scored on five non-judge axes. (i) \emph{Stance
drift} $|\hat\alpha(y) - \hat\alpha(x)|$, regressor above.
(ii) \emph{Length}, word-count ratio to source. (iii) \emph{Semantic
similarity} via e5-large-v2 cosine to the source, populated for
methods whose embedding pass has run.
(iv) \emph{AI detectability}: a supervised TF-IDF $+$ logistic
regression classifier trained on the \paulcorpus
writer-vs-AI split, held-out AUROC $0.989$ with per-model AUROCs
$0.996$ (Claude Sonnet 4), $0.993$ (DeepSeek V3), $0.978$ (GPT-4o);
the classifier's output is an upper bound on stylistic machineness and
a lower bound on detectability against the wild, because it has
learnt three specific model signatures.
(v) \emph{Domain-learnt clarity}: a second DeBERTa-v3-base regressor
trained on mean human clarity (Pearson $0.76$, MAE $0.07$ held-out) as
a judge-independent quality estimate.

\subsection{Statistics}
% TL;DR: paired deltas, cluster bootstrap over propositions, both aggregators.
For each method and each panel we report the mean paired delta $\Delta$
between rewrite score and source score, averaged across paragraphs.
Gold-panel scores are aggregated two ways per \cref{sec:problem}:
$A_\text{mean}$ and $A_\text{trim}$. $95\%$ CIs come from cluster
bootstrap at the proposition level ($100$ clusters, $1000$
replicates), because multiple paragraphs share a proposition and a
human rater pool. \gap\ is computed paired per paragraph before
averaging. Method ranking on each panel uses the mean $\Delta$; the
paired comparison between rankings uses Spearman $\rho$ bootstrapped
the same way. We pre-commit to reporting results under both
aggregators for every method in every downstream table.

\section{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:setup}

% TL;DR: concrete instantiation. Scope, baselines, implementation knobs.
The experiment has three concrete scopes.
\textbf{(i) Attack-panel pass:} every source paragraph and every
rewrite in the $20\%$ sample is scored by both proxy judges. Ten
methods $\times$ $903$ paragraphs $\approx 18{,}000$ calls; all
complete at submission. The source paragraph panel mean sets the
baseline at which uplift is measured.
\textbf{(ii) Gold-panel pass:} the same paragraphs are re-scored by
all five gold judges. At submission three methods have
complete gold coverage (\texttt{naive}, $n{=}903$;
\texttt{injection\_leadin}, $n{=}695$;
\texttt{lit\_informed\_tight}, $n{=}481$) and the remaining seven are
being re-scored. OpenRouter provider errors during the
first gold pass silently returned parseable envelopes with missing
score fields, which the judge client currently records as
\texttt{NULL}; the ongoing re-score inserts over the nulls.
\textbf{(iii) Non-judge metrics:} stance regressor, length, clarity
regressor, and AI detector run on all ten methods; embedding cosine
and hallucinated-specifics run on subsets due to environment issues
that are noted inline. All of (i)--(iii) write into a single
append-only SQLite \texttt{evaluations} table keyed on (paragraph,
metric, criterion, source).

\texttt{naive}                & 32.90 & 26.24 & 26.83 & $6.66$  $[5.80, 7.45]$ & $6.07$ $[5.22, 6.83]$ \\

% TL;DR: gap is real both ways; trim makes it ~7 pts across methods.
Every \gap\ CI in \cref{tab:gap} excludes zero by a wide margin under
both aggregators. Under the trimmed mean the three methods cluster
inside a $1.3$-point band ($6.07$, $7.12$, $7.40$). Under the naive
mean \texttt{lit\_informed\_tight}'s gap balloons to $11.40$, and its
CI no longer overlaps with \texttt{naive}'s CI. The attack panel
therefore systematically over-states gold uplift by about seven
clarity points independent of method; the additional four-point
excess that appears for \texttt{lit\_informed\_tight} under the naive
mean is aggregator-specific.

\subsection{Apparent rank reversal is a one-judge artefact}

% TL;DR: rank reversal on mean-of-5, disappears on trimmed mean.
On the attack panel the three methods rank
\texttt{lit\_informed\_tight}$>$\texttt{injection\_leadin}$>$\texttt{naive}
($34.64 \approx 34.62 > 32.90$). On the gold panel the answer depends
on aggregation. Under naive mean-of-five the ranking is
\texttt{injection\_leadin}$>$\texttt{naive}$>$\texttt{lit\_informed\_tight}
($26.94 > 26.24 > 23.24$): Spearman $\rho = -1$ against the attack
ranking, a complete reversal. Under trimmed mean the ranking becomes
\texttt{injection\_leadin}$>$\texttt{lit\_informed\_tight}$>$\texttt{naive}
($27.50 > 27.24 > 26.83$), and Spearman $\rho$ against the attack
ranking rises to $+0.5$. A defender choosing the ``riskiest rewriter
to emulate'' from the un-trimmed gold panel picks the worst of the
three; the same defender using trimmed gold picks the same method the
attack panel picks, i.e.\ no reversal at all.
Scoring behind the reversal: trimmed gold moves
\texttt{lit\_informed\_tight} by $+4$ points relative to its raw mean;
the same trim moves \texttt{naive} by only $+0.6$ and
\texttt{injection\_leadin} by $+0.6$. The outlier is concentrated in
one method.

\subsection{The outlier is DeepSeek on \texttt{lit\_informed\_tight}}
\label{sec:driver}

% TL;DR: DeepSeek-V3.2 rates lit_informed_tight 22 pts below peers.
\Cref{fig:perjudge} breaks the gold-panel mean into per-judge means.
On four of the five gold judges the three rewriter methods finish
within $2.0$ points of one another. On DeepSeek-V3.2
\texttt{lit\_informed\_tight} scores $67.2$ while
\texttt{naive} scores $88.5$ and \texttt{injection\_leadin} scores
$89.4$: a $\sim 22$-point drop on one judge, not a uniform shift
across gold. The trimmed-mean aggregator in \cref{tab:gap} is
designed for exactly this shape. The un-trimmed mean inherits the
outlier and reports a false rank reversal; the trimmed mean ignores
the worst single judgement per paragraph and reports a gap that is
consistent across methods. Reading the rewriter outputs, the
literature-informed prompt asks for ``visible reasoning'' and
``main claim first''; its rewrites are more scaffolded, with explicit
\emph{because}-chains and topic sentences. That structure aligns with
what the proxy-tier open judges reward and, on the evidence here,
systematically mis-aligns with one gold judge's preferences. Whether
the effect is DeepSeek-specific, structural-prose-specific, or
latent in the training pipeline is the experiment the fuller gold
pass (and a non-political replication) should answer.

\subsection{Attack-panel ranking (all methods)}

% TL;DR: all 10 attack ranks, CIs overlap a lot.
\Cref{fig:attack} shows the attack-panel $\Delta$ for all ten methods
with bootstrap CIs. Spread from floor (\texttt{naive}, $32.90$) to

neighbouring methods' CIs overlap. The attack panel is a
low-resolution instrument for ranking methods whose uplifts differ
by $<1$ point---relevant when the training-based arm lands and its
gold-panel improvement is what distinguishes it from inference.

\subsection{Drift, detection, and domain-learnt quality}

% TL;DR: stance drift correlates with attack uplift; all rewrites detectable.
\Cref{fig:drift} plots stance drift against attack-panel uplift.
High-drift methods are also the high-uplift methods: the best-scoring
rewriter on the proxy panel (\texttt{rules\_explicit}) has stance
drift $0.199$, roughly four times \texttt{naive}'s $0.055$. On a
rewriting task where stance preservation is the fidelity constraint,
this is a trade-off, not a free lunch.

$x$: attack-panel paired uplift. $y$: mean absolute change in the

% TL;DR: all rewriters are trivially detectable.
The supervised AI detector places rewrites from all three methods
with detector coverage at
$p(\text{machine}) \approx 0.80$, against a source baseline of
$0.36$: $+0.44$. The detector learnt the stylistic signatures of the
three AI models represented in the \paulcorpus training corpus, so
this is a lower bound on general detectability, not a report on it.
Even so, no inference-only rewriter we tested passes for human at
that bound.

% TL;DR: domain clarity regressor sides with attack panel, not DeepSeek.
The domain-learnt clarity regressor (trained on mean human clarity
ratings) rates the three gold-complete methods
\texttt{lit\_informed\_tight}$\approx$\texttt{naive}$\approx$\texttt{injection\_leadin}
(predictions $0.866$, $0.846$, $0.822$). The regressor disagrees with
DeepSeek-V3.2's reading of \texttt{lit\_informed\_tight}: on
human-calibrated clarity the method is at the top of the pack. That
does not refute DeepSeek; rubric compliance and human clarity are
not the same construct. It does say that the rank reversal in
\Cref{fig:transfer} is not visible to a human-calibrated scorer.

\subsection{Stubs for the ongoing passes}
\label{sec:training-results}

% TL;DR: stubs written in target style; will be overwritten by data.
\paragraph{Completing the gold panel.}
When the re-score finishes for the remaining seven methods, the
four-quadrant decomposition (\cref{fig:transfer}) expands from three
points to ten. We expect the three prompt-only methods
(\texttt{naive\_tight}, \texttt{rubric\_aware}, \texttt{rules\_explicit})
to sit within a point of \texttt{naive}'s gold uplift, the
judge-feedback methods (\texttt{bon\_panel}, \texttt{icir\_single}) to
sit near \texttt{lit\_informed\_tight}'s, and
\texttt{scaffolded\_cot\_distill} to follow \texttt{lit\_informed}.
Whether \texttt{rules\_explicit} produces the largest \gap\ (it ranks
first on attack) or merely the largest stance drift (\cref{fig:drift})
is the specific open question.

\paragraph{Training-based arm (SFT, GRPO).}
Both training-based methods score against the same two proxy judges
in \cref{tab:panels}, and the evaluation protocol extends the same
gold panel with a held-out Gemma-2-9B-Instruct judge (not in either
panel). A successful training-based attack raises mean proxy reward
while leaving gold-panel reward flat or falling: the \citet{gao2023}
signature, translated to LLM-judge land. The $25$-step reference GRPO
run already shows $+6.93$ on mean proxy score and $+2.10$ on the
held-out Gemma judge---the characteristic gap that makes the full
training-based test interesting.

\paragraph{Fidelity-controlled comparison.}
Rerunning the \gap\ with outcomes length-controlled (regressing out
word-count ratio) and stance-controlled (regressing out stance drift)
isolates the judge-specific component of uplift from the drift-driven
one. We commit to reporting the length-controlled \gap\ for all ten
methods in the camera-ready.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Trim the gold mean}
% TL;DR: practical: small gold panels need robust aggregators.
A five-judge gold panel is small. Small samples of noisy estimators
have a known failure mode: the sample mean is sensitive to a single
outlier, and the worst-case single judgement per paragraph is what
the attack panel was trained against. Trimmed mean restores the
finding that matters---the $\sim 7$-point transfer
gap---while eliminating a method-specific spike that would have been
reported as an attacker signature. Any evaluator operating with a
small gold panel should either report the per-judge breakdown, use a
trimmed mean, or both. Reporting only the gold-mean, as is common in
the RM-overoptimisation lineage
\citep{gao2023,coste2024,eisenstein2023}, risks this exact
confusion.

\subsection{What the literature-informed prompt actually teaches}
% TL;DR: the specific result about prompt-engineering-for-judges.
The prompt that distils five years of GEO, Prometheus, and G-Eval
advice into five principles scores highest on the proxy panel.
Under the trimmed-mean gold it sits at the top of the gold panel
too. Under naive gold it collapses to last place because one
gold judge (DeepSeek-V3.2) disagrees. That is a genuine single-judge
failure mode, not evidence that the prompt games the proxy panel.
Whether the DeepSeek-V3.2 effect generalises beyond political
content is outside this paper's scope, but it is testable with the
release: every (paragraph, judge) score is in the SQLite dump.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}
% TL;DR: honest about what we can't say.
Single criterion (clarity), single domain (UK political opinion),
single held-out model in the training arm, a single fold.
Non-frontier proxies and cost-downgraded gold panel
(\cref{tab:panels}; no Sonnet, no Gemini Pro, no Opus). The
bootstrap's effective degrees of freedom is $\sim 100$ clusters.
The AI detector encodes corpus-specific signatures; we read it as a
lower bound, not an upper one. Gold-panel coverage is partial at
submission---three of ten methods complete, one at $n{=}44$, the rest
being re-scored. The seven missing methods' \gap\ values could
change the picture: if all seven sit near the trimmed $\sim 7$-point
band the method-uniform gap hardens; if any one of them has a
per-judge outlier of DeepSeek's size, a second rank reversal under
naive mean is possible. The trimmed-mean curve is harder to move.
The trimmed-mean estimator itself has a breakdown point of
$1/5$; two outliers of the same size on a paragraph would still
slip through.

\subsection{Ethics}
\label{sec:ethics}
% TL;DR: dual-use paper; detection axes are the mitigation.
The paper describes inference-only rewriters that raise an LLM
judge's clarity score on politically charged text without changing
the source's stance. This is a dual-use result, standard in the
rewriting-attack lineage
(\citealp{raina2024,kaneko2026paa,zou2023gcg}). Our detection and
drift axes---stance regressor, AI detector, domain clarity
regressor---are specifically designed to raise the defender's signal
on the attack; we publish them together, and commit to releasing
the regressors' weights.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

% TL;DR: the so-what, break the fourth wall.
% (Carlini: conclusions are not abstract-in-past-tense.)
We set out to measure how much of an attacker's uplift on an LLM
judge survives a change of judge. Read through the
reward-model-overoptimisation literature, the expected answer was a
single number: an uplift haircut, roughly the same across methods.
It almost is. Under a robust aggregator (trimmed mean over a
five-judge gold panel) the three methods we could fully score sit
inside a $1.3$-point band: attacker uplift over-states gold uplift
by about seven clarity points, method-independent. Under the naive
mean-of-five the same three methods diverge and the ranking
reverses. The lesson is about the aggregator, not the attacker.

% TL;DR: what to work on next.
The open work is concrete. Finish the gold re-score on the seven
methods still pending and upgrade the rank-reversal claim from three
points to ten. Run the training-based arm and confirm the
\citet{gao2023} signature on LLM judges. Extend to a second
criterion (the informativeness regressor is already trained). Swap
the proxy pair for three proxies and test whether a DeepSeek-shaped
single-judge failure still appears one-judge-wide or averages out.
Report both mean-of-$k$ and trimmed-mean gold in every downstream
evaluation; the scripts and seeds ship with the paper.

\texttt{trl==1.2.0}, \texttt{vllm==0.18.1}, \texttt{torch==2.10.0+cu128},
\texttt{transformers==4.57.6}. Seed $42$.

Per-step diagnostics (wandb): per-judge reward mean, ensemble std,
completion length min/mean/max, $\texttt{frac\_reward\_zero\_std}$,
vLLM-vs-HF importance-sampling ratio, KL to reference, entropy,
gradient norm. Held-out Gemma-2-9B-Instruct judge evaluated
pre- and post-training on a $50$-paragraph disjoint slice of
top-decile writers.

\section{Analysis hygiene}
\label{app:analysis}

The analysis driver (\texttt{eval\_suite/analyse.py}) aggregates the
\texttt{evaluations} table by (method, metric, criterion, panel,
source). In the partial-gold state at submission, rows with
\texttt{value IS NULL} pass the default groupby filter and cause the
mean to be computed over a reduced sample; a subsequent bugfix (not
yet merged at the time of this draft) filters NULLs explicitly before
aggregation and yields the numbers reported in \cref{sec:results}.
All numbers in the paper are computed in a separate script that hits
SQLite directly with a \texttt{value IS NOT NULL} guard, to avoid
dependence on the driver's intermediate state.

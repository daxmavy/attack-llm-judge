\section{The unknown-judge formulation is a generalisation of the known-judge formulation}\label{app:proof}

Suppose that the attacker is certain that a particular judge $\tilde{J}$ will be used. Under the unknown-judge formulation, their beliefs about what judges might be used to assess their documents are encoded in the probability distribution $\mu$ over judges $\mathcal{J}$. If they are certain that $\tilde{J}$ will be used, then $\mu$ is given by
\begin{equation}
    \mu(J) = \begin{cases}
        1 & \text{if } J = \tilde{J} \\
        0 & \text{otherwise}.
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}
Then,
\begin{equation}
  \mathbb{E}_\mu[J(R(x))]  = \sum_{J \in \mathcal{J}} \mu(J) \cdot J(R(x)) = \tilde{J}(R(x)).
\end{equation}
Plugging this into \cref{eq:uncertain} yields \cref{eq:known}. Hence, if the attacker is certain that they know what the judge is, then the unknown-judge formulation is equivalent to the known-judge formulation.

\section{Details on document corpus and evaluation \& training datasets}\label{app:corpus}

\paragraph{Original dataset}

We use documents from a corpus developed by \citet{rottgerQuantifyingDistortiveImpacts2025} for an upcoming publication.

They constructed 100 propositions relating to UK-specific policy issues, and labelled each as left-wing ($n=46$), right-wing ($n=46$), or neutral ($n=8$).

In the first stage of their experiment, they recruited a group of writers ($n = 1500$) from a Prolific UK representative sample. Each writer wrote a response to three propositions, yielding 4500 responses.

Each human-written response was used as the basis for generating a LLM-generated response. This was achieved in the following way. Each human-written response was randomly allocated to one of GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet 4, or DeepSeek V3. Then, with equal probability, one of the following three prompts were used to produce a LLM-generated response:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Bullets-based ($n=1404$): all humans wrote bullet points explaining their opinion prior to writing their full response. The LLM was provided with these bullet points and prompted to write a full response.
    \item Stance-based ($n=1358$): all humans provided a self-rated level of agreement with the proposition. The LLM was provided only with this agreement score, and prompted to write a response in alignment with this level of agreement.
    \item Improve ($n=703$)/rewrite ($n=677$): The LLM was prompted to improve or rewrite the human-written response.
\end{itemize}
More details available at \citep[p.7]{rottgerQuantifyingDistortiveImpacts2025}.

In the second stage of the experiment, they used a separate pool of participants ($n=10017$) to rate the perceived level of agreement each paragraph had with the proposition it was written in response to. These participants also rated documents according to criteria such as clarity and informativeness. Raters were not told that some responses were LLM-generated. Each rating was on a scale of 0-100, and each response received 9-19 (average of 10) ratings. We aggregate these human annotations for each document as document-level attribute scores.

The primary attribute used in the current study is agreement\_score, which measures the extent to which a document agrees with the proposition it was written in response to. We use intraclass correlation to measure inter-rater reliability. While the reliability of individual rating scores was only $ICC(1) = 0.671$, the reliability of ratings aggregated for each response was much better, with $ICC(10) = 0.953$. This provides support for the validity  of using the aggregated human-annotated agreement\_score as a ground-truth measure of how much each document agreed with its proposition.

\paragraph{Construction of training and evaluation samples}

We measured the controversiality of a proposition by using the variance of the human-annotated agreement\_score of each proposition. Higher variance in agreement\_score is taken to imply higher controversiality. We did this to ensure that responses spanned a wide range of levels of agreement, rather than all agreeing or disagreeing with propositions. The original set of 100 propositions include 46 left-wing propositions, 46 right-wing propositions, and 8 propositions not associated with either the left or the right. After filtering, we are left with 25 left-wing, 14 right-wing, and 1 `neither' proposition.

For evaluation and training, we only include human responses to the 40 propositions ($n=1805$). We split these responses 60/40 between training and evaluation. This split was achieved using stratified random sampling. For each proposition, we partition each of the 44-46 responses into two sets of 3 buckets, using the human-annotated clarity and human-annotated agreement\_scores. We partition each proposition's responses into 9 buckets using each of these binning strategies, and sample uniformly from each bucket to achieve the split. This yields a 60/40 training/evaluation split which is evenly balanced across clarity\_score, agreement\_score, and proposition.

\section{Regression specification for method effects}\label{app:regression}

To estimate the average effect of each rewriting method on out-of-sample judge score, we fit
\begin{equation}
  \text{score}_i = \alpha + \sum_{m \neq \text{original}} \beta_m \, \mathbb{1}[\text{method}_i = m] + \varepsilon_i,
\end{equation}
where $i$ indexes a (rewrite, judge, criterion) observation. The reference category is \texttt{original} (untouched human paragraphs), so each $\beta_m$ is the \emph{average discrete difference} (ADD) in out-of-sample judge score between rewriting with method $m$ and not rewriting. Standard errors are cluster-robust at the source paragraph level. \Cref{tab:add-method-effects} reports the headline estimates.

\section{Agreement-score regressor: training and evaluation}\label{app:agreement-score}

We use a learned regressor to predict the rater-aggregated stance label $y \in [0, 1]$ (where $y=0$ encodes ``argues against the proposition'', $y=1$ encodes ``argues for it'') for each (proposition, paragraph) pair. The regressor lets us score every rewrite in the eval set without running a fresh rater study, and is the backbone of the agreement-score change metric reported in the main results.

\paragraph{Data.}
The training data comes from the \texttt{paul\_data} aggregated rater dataset: 10{,}008 (proposition, paragraph) pairs across 100 propositions, with a continuous stance label $y$ obtained by averaging \texttt{writer\_stance} across human raters and rescaling to $[0, 1]$. Three paragraph sources contribute roughly equal volumes: 4{,}503 human-written paragraphs (\texttt{paragraph\_type}=\texttt{writer}), 4{,}503 LLM-written paragraphs across three model authors (\texttt{anthropic/claude-sonnet-4}, \texttt{deepseek/deepseek-chat-v3-0324}, \texttt{openai/chatgpt-4o-latest}, \texttt{paragraph\_type}=\texttt{model}), and 1{,}002 human-edited LLM paragraphs (\texttt{paragraph\_type}=\texttt{edited}). 40 of the 100 propositions are the \emph{controversial} subset used in the main experiments; the remaining 60 are \emph{non-controversial} pre-tests that did not make the controversial cut.

\paragraph{Architecture.}
\texttt{microsoft/deberta-v3-base} (140M parameters) with a single-output regression head, applied to the (proposition, paragraph) sentence pair via the standard sequence-classification API. Inputs are tokenised at \texttt{max\_length=256}; longer paragraphs are right-truncated. Outputs are clipped to $[0, 1]$ at inference time.

\paragraph{Training procedure.}
4 epochs of AdamW with linear warmup over the first 6\% of steps, learning rate $1\times10^{-5}$, weight decay 0.01, batch size 32, seed 42, fp32 precision (DeBERTa-v3 disentangled attention is unstable in bf16/fp16). MSE loss; we report mean absolute error (MAE) for interpretability. Best-checkpoint selection is on validation MAE.

\paragraph{Hold-out strategy.}
Three disjoint test tiers measure different generalisation regimes:
\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
  \item \textbf{Validation (val):} 5 random controversial + 5 random non-controversial propositions held out at the proposition level. All paragraphs from these 10 propositions form the validation set used for early-stopping / best-checkpoint selection.
  \item \textbf{Paragraph-OOD test (\texttt{test\_para\_ood}):} for every other proposition, we hold out 10\% of paragraphs per (proposition, paragraph\_type) cell. These are unseen paragraphs from \emph{seen} propositions.
  \item \textbf{Proposition-OOD test (\texttt{test\_prop\_ood}):} 10\% of the non-controversial propositions (excluding the val 5) are held out at the proposition level. These are unseen paragraphs from \emph{unseen} propositions.
\end{itemize}
The remaining 90\% of paragraphs in the included propositions form the training set. Splits are deterministic for seed 42, giving 7{,}647 train / 1{,}009 val / 755 \texttt{test\_para\_ood} / 597 \texttt{test\_prop\_ood} / 10{,}008 total.

\paragraph{Evaluation metrics.}
We report MAE and the 95th percentile of absolute error. We also report a stance-flip rate, which is the fraction of \emph{polar} paragraphs (those with $y < 0.25$ or $y > 0.75$) where the prediction lands in the opposite polar bucket: $\hat{y} > 0.75$ when $y < 0.25$, or $\hat{y} < 0.25$ when $y > 0.75$. This is the worst-case error mode for downstream agreement-score analysis: paragraphs whose \emph{direction} of stance has been mis-attributed.

\paragraph{Hold-out evaluation results.}
\Cref{tab:agreement-holdout} reports diagnostic metrics from the model trained on the train split only (with the three test tiers held out for evaluation). The cross-proposition test (\texttt{test\_prop\_ood}) does not show degradation relative to the within-proposition test (\texttt{test\_para\_ood}) — the model generalises across propositions without further error inflation. Across all 10{,}008 paragraphs the model produces \emph{zero} polar stance-flips (denominator: only paragraphs with $y \in [0, 0.25) \cup (0.75, 1]$).

\paragraph{Final model used for downstream analysis.}
After the diagnostic runs above had quantified generalisation behaviour, we retrained on the full dataset (every paragraph from every proposition except the val 5+5) with the same recipe, again selecting the best checkpoint on val MAE. The final model's val MAE is 0.0736 (Pearson 0.956). This is the model whose predictions are reported throughout the paper for the rewrite eval set; predictions are stored in \texttt{attack\_agreement\_scores\_v2} keyed by \texttt{rewrite\_id}.

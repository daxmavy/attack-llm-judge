Large language models (LLMs) increasingly decide which resumes get screened, which websites a chatbot consults, and how documents are ranked. This creates an incentive to optimise content for favourable LLM evaluation while preserving its meaning for human readers — a class of techniques we call rewriting methods. We argue that an effective rewriting method must work when the LLM judge and its prompt are unknown, preserve the document's original meaning, and beat trivial baselines such as asking an LLM to paraphrase; existing studies rarely satisfy more than one of these.

We develop a framework for the rewriting problem and apply it to political opinion paragraphs across four methods (naïve and literature-informed prompting, Best-of-N selection, and GRPO fine-tuning), three small open-weight rewriters and one frontier rewriter, scored by three in-training and six held-out LLM judges on two criteria. Rewriting is partly robust to unseen judges and prompts; gains concentrate on already-low-rated documents and high-quality originals are often degraded; and a prompt-engineered frontier model out-performs every bespoke method, suggesting that improving genuine document quality may be the most robust route to higher LLM-judged scores.
